    Introduction to deduction     A deduction (also called an inference) is a kind of statement that needs some hypotheses to be true in order for its conclusion to be true. A theorem, on the other hand, has no hypotheses (or all its hypotheses are definition). (Informally we may call both of them theorems, but on this page we will stick to the strict definition.)

    When being rigorous, it is surprisingly complicated to convert some deduction in the form        into some theorem in the form . The deduction says, "if we can prove then we can prove ," which is in some sense weaker than saying " implies ." In particular, there is no axiom of logic that permits us to directly obtain the theorem given the deduction. The conversion of a deduction to a theorem does not even hold in general for quantum propositional calculus, which is a weak subset of classical propositional calculus. It has been shown that adding the Standard Deduction Theorem (below) to quantum propositional calculus turns it into classical propositional calculus!

    This is in contrast to going the other way; if we have the theorem, it is easy to recover the deduction using modus ponens ax-mp. An example of this is the derivation of the inference simpli from the theorem simpl.

    In traditional logic books, this is often resolved using a metatheorem called the Deduction Theorem (discovered independently by Herbrand and Tarski around 1930). The deduction theorem, aka the standard deduction theorem, provides an algorithm for constructing a proof of a theorem from the proof of its corresponding deduction. See, for example, Margaris, First Order Mathematical Logic, p. 56. To construct a proof for a theorem, the deduction theorem's algorithm looks at each step in the proof of the original deduction and rewrites the step with several steps wherein the hypothesis is eliminated and becomes an antecedent. Ordinary mathematical proofs invoke this algorithm when converting a deduction into a theorem.

    However, in practice, no one actually carries out this conversion algorithm in ordinary mathematics. Instead, mathematicians often stop once they believe the conversion could be done. This sharply contrasts with Metamath's philosophical goal, which is to show all proofs directly from the axiom system in its database, and not skip steps or rely on higher-level metatheorems derived outside of that axiom system.

    Metamath is capable of converting a hypothesis into an antecedent. We have found that in many cases where this is desired it easier to do this using a weakened form of the deduction theorem, called the weak deduction theorem (dedth and its variants). For more information using the deduction theorem and the weak deduction theorem, see the deduction theorem page.

    Current approach    

    Using the deduction theorem, or a weak variant of it, is rarely used today in MPE when creating new proofs.

    Current MPE conventions instead encourage using forms that are easier to deal with directly. In particular, there is a preference for instead writing assertions in "deduction form" (applying this approach is called "deduction style"). In an assertion in deduction form, the conclusion is prefixed with , and every hypothesis is a definition or is also prefixed with . In this form the antecedent mimics the context in a deduction proof system. For more information, see "Natural Deductions in the Metamath Proof Language" by Mario Carneiro, 2014.

    Many other theorems are presented as "tautologies" or "tautology form" (a form with no hypotheses or where all hypotheses are definitions). Tautologies are not deductions and thus require no special treatment. These forms are much easier to deal with directly in Metamath as compared to deductions, produce clearer proofs. simplifies proof reuse, and leads to fewer situations where the deduction theorem (and its variants) are needed.

    Natural Deduction     It is possible to develop proofs in MPE that follow the style Gentzen's natural deduction rules. In short, use deduction form, and apply MPE theorems that are their logical equivalent.
